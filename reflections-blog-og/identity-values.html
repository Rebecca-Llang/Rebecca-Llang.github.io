Ethical Decisions

A recent ethical decision I made was how I utilise my time and capacity, and the role I play within my community. I decided to reassess and disengage myself from certain environments. Doing this and re-prioritising my goals, was an incredibly hard decision to make. I felt a personal responsibility to use my skills to improve the spaces and communities I belonged to, particularly when it came to bringing people together, creating a productive and safe environment and making sure those who struggle to share or be heard, are heard. .

During a conference many years ago on environmental organising, the simple sentence “some need to take a step back, so others can take a step forward” had a massive impact on me. It reframed the way I saw my engagement as an assertive and social person. It made me rethink the ways in which empowerment, autonomy and active participation move within any group with a shared goal. It helps me slow down, hear and understand perspectives, so we can grow and build together. A much later realisation was that someone else can step into the role that I leave.

Making the choice to withdraw my active participation in some spaces felt like I was betraying myself and my communities. As a queer wahine, It felt like I was giving up, being selfish and leaving people who needed me behind. It felt like I was choosing the individual over the collective, comfort over improvement, and participating in the problem, rather than the solution. I felt a lot of confusion over the right choice to make, however I also knew I needed to make some changes to move forward within a personal capacity.

I had to shift focus, put my own oxygen mask on first, and apply that focus to improving and finding balance in my own life. I learnt how to say no to the people and things that I loved, and maintain my boundaries, so I could discover and create the life I needed to move forward, so that I can better serve my whanau and communities in a sustainable way.

The way in which I was applying my personal ethics and capacity was unsustainable, and prevented me from being able to focus on my personal goals. Re-evaluating my capacity, having trust in others and finding new ways to live allowed me to make decisions about my own future. It was the right decision. I still help in the small capacity that I can, and I am excited to bring the same enthusiasm, with a deeper understanding of myself, to other areas of my life.

Whanau and Culture

My father would say “always stand up for the underdog” and “do what makes you happy” and I took both of these extremely seriously and applied it fully to my life. Primarily coming from Otepoti/Dunedin, from a big bougie bogan family, we often had different experiences and understandings of the world and the problems within it.

We spent our working in the family businesses, playing in nature, and spent a lot of time in heated debate. Nothing was off the table to be discussed, however it was often in a very disrespectful manner, and very in line with colonial thinking. This taught me de-escalation and how complicated interpersonal relationships can be, and the role in which I play in them. It also fostered a strong understanding of what I want, and practice, in my own communities and relationships.

This was very different to the time I spent with my mother, whom I had lived with on and off in England and Tauranga. She existed in a very different space and experience of this world, one that I have always felt I needed to defend and protect. She did not have the same support and opportunities that I have received in my life. I have always been shocked at the way she was treated or dismissed in so many different environments. Having this juxtaposition within my up-bringing, flying back and forth between them, and trying my best to make sure my siblings were safe and comfortable, explains a lot about who I am and the values I hold. For me, home has always been the people I love.

Strengths and My Learning Journey

Love, Appreciation, Kindness, Gratitude and Curiosity are my biggest strengths as it translates to enthusiastic and compassionate engagement.

My personal strengths results from allright.org.nz's Strengths Finder

I found my results from allright.org.nz's Strengths Finder interesting as I feel like my behaviour is very contextual to the specific situation. It was difficult to feel like it covered the nuance of being a human into today’s world. My Extraversion and Openness to Experience both ranked high. These traits have always been a strong part of my identity and experience. I always find it interesting when I don’t behave this way, as I then can check what is causing me anxiety, or if there is a misalignment in my life that is causing me to retreat. Like all things in life, we are always changing and growing. Having more self-control over these aspects of myself has helped me find more balance. I am forever grateful that I am who I am, and that I exist in the way that I do. It allows me to meet people, make connections and learn from interactions constantly. It brings me joy every single day. It has helped me overcome many barriers in my life and has always given me hope about a better future for us all.

My neuroticism results from allright.org.nz's Strengths Finder

The Neuroticism traits were interesting to me. I was surprised my anxiety and self-consciousness was low, as I feel anxiety in settings where I’m unsure how my enthusiasm and energy will be received, or if I am potentially in danger of being put in an uncomfortable or unsafe situation. When I share my anxieties or reflections, people who don’t know me well are shocked that I feel that way. I see how it can be at odds with how I present, as I am often seen as extremely confident and secure. This is another reason that I strongly value making sure people feel safe, and heard. I feel anger when people are malicious or bully others, for life is hard enough and healing can only come from love.

My agreeableness results from allright.org.nz's Strengths Finder

I was also surprised that agreeableness was so high. I strive for balance and fairness, for all. However, I do not consider myself someone who would betray myself, for the comfort, or unaccountability, of one over the collective. Individual needs must be met, but not cause harm to others. I am not modest as a person, but I am open about my mistakes and weaknesses. Which I consider a strength. I try to be tactful, but I believe self-compassion and appreciation is important. A good friend of mine once said “She always thinks she is amazing, but she also thinks everyone else in the room is, too”, and I thought that was a beautiful way to describe it. I find it really hard to be modest, for life isn’t easy and I wouldn’t be here today if it wasn’t for the steps I’ve taken, work I’ve done and the support and love I’ve received from the people in my life, and I love to celebrate that.

Limitations

Perseverance, Prudence, Humility and Self-Regulation and disruptive ADHD symptoms are my biggest challenges.

My conscientiouness results from allright.org.nz's Strengths Finder

I strive for balance but I also love to treat myself in whatever capacity I can. Due to my dopamine chasing nature, I’ve set many boundaries and limitations around what, how and why I engage with situations and hobbies. I love a good chat, and will talk to anyone, so things like headphones, actively opening with the prelude that I have something else to do/or have to go, and tactfully seizing the energy to leave as soon as it hits helps.

All in all, I feel like everything adds up and I am living a healthy life. I am proud of the steps I have taken to find stability within myself. Medication, balance of schedule, planning and whanau support help immensely.

I try to have compassion for myself when I lose something or make a bad call with time-management, or feel anxiety over how something was interpreted. I try to keep the frustration or sadness fleeting. Looking at nature, the people I love, or something colourful always helps. In these moments, I try to remember that I am just an animal on this earth trying to survive under a system that is not built for everyone. I use calendars, alarms, notes, routines and my whare is very specific in its functionality so that my ADHD traits don’t bring too much chaos. Time-blindness and high emotion are two things that I have to keep in check every single day. I tend to feel deeply, and luckily it’s usually joy. You can’t ignore your feelings, only manage them. Receiving my ADHD diagnosis, medication and therapy have greatly helped me.

I have a range of ways to self soothe, communicate and breathe. I appreciate that my high energy translates into a lot of unintentional healthy behaviours and habits, as long as I keep coaxing them in that direction. I would have loved to have had that support as a child and young adult. I have always worked hard to prove myself, achieved most of my goals and have, and had, the most incredible friends. I have always been extremely grateful for my chosen whanau, however It wasn’t until I actively separated myself and had the blessing of being able to live alone for a while, that I realised how much we had learnt from each other and how much I depended on them. Community holds each other up when the system fails. Community is the system that matters to me the most. I would not be as capable as I am, if it wasn’t for their unwavering love and support. Making the choice to take a step back, allowed me to build the systems I need to thrive independently, so I can sustainably participate within the group.

Strategies Around Productive Collaberation

Conflict resolution is a skill I've spent my whole life refining, coming from a large family who worked and lived together throughout my early youth, and from many of my mahi choices.

A recent example was when we had a big change in our shared working space, with a new collaborator who had very different values and work ethic to the rest of our space. The traditional channels that were set up, such as direct feedback and meetings to discuss issues, were unproductive, with no change or if so, little adherence to it. This caused a lot of disruption and made it very difficult for some to participate. We decided to create a workers collective, so that we could clearly and effectively all work together and prevent further disentanglement and alienation.

During these meetings, I was struggling with not deviating from the agenda and debating with a specific person. We agreed on the problems, but had very different ideas about appropriate solutions. It would get very cyclical, with neither of us really hearing each other, and although we had the passion for it, it was unproductive and noticeably interrupting the flow and time constraints we had, as well as being tiresome for those who would have to wait for us to get our points across. When I clocked the cycle we were in, and that we weren’t going to be able to move forward or make a decision, as well as causing discomfort to the rest of our peers, we tried multiple actions, including:

  • Putting a pin in it to return to later when it was appropriate, or more relevant. This was helpful for the agenda, however sometimes meant the issue was lost.
  • Reiterating our goals and needs prior to engagement, this helped everyone be more present and mindful.
  • Us both discussing particulars outside of meetings, particularly before meetings, so that we had the space to hear and learn from each other, without it stagnating the goals of our collective. This was effective but time consuming, and had a wonderful impact on our interpersonal relationship.
  • We also shifted back to the collective space, as we’d been hosting in our homes and third places, and found it kept us more focused and structured. The behaviours and issues we were experiencing were at odds with the very well defined kaupapa we shared within that space, and this was very helpful.

The thing I found most interesting was we were both incredibly invested and passionate, very much on the same team, however had different ideas of the stakeholders, resources and effective strategy. The group would comment that we would both be saying the same thing, however in different ways, which made witnessing these interactions even more frustrating for them. This was helpful, as it was clearly a blindspot for us both. We started to focus on clarifying questions and understanding the message when the other was talking. We would acknowledge we were activated, self-regulate, and then return if we noticed ourselves slipping back into that pattern. Transparency and accountability was strong throughout this process, as we all know that strength is found in the collective, and that took priority over our egos. We also had a lot of respect for each other, and had very important roles to play within the collective. I learnt so much from her and from the mahi we put in to be able to collaborate. Both theoretically, and how to apply de-escalation and compassion to myself and others, even when I feel frustration and such high stakes. If I had the chance to try other strategies, it would have been to identify what I can control and not let transference of previous experiences feed into the present, activating defensiveness within myself.
