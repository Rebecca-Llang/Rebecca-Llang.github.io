What is Emotional Intelligence?

Emotional intelligence (EQ) is the active practice and understanding of emotions and perspectives. This involves not only understanding our own emotions and behaviours but also recognizing and empathising with the feelings and viewpoints of others. EQ is about identifying and processing our own emotions, while also being attuned to the emotions and perspectives of those around us. Applying compassion, communicating clearly, and holding oneself accountable—without becoming defensive—are all key components of emotional intelligence. These skills are cultivated and honed through continuous practice, both within ourselves and in our interactions with others.

EQ & IQ

EQ differs from IQ in its focus and skillset. IQ is traditionally associated with memory, logic, reasoning, and the application of knowledge in areas like the sciences. It is often seen as a measure of cognitive processing and performance.

However, this view can be limiting, as it tends to overlook other forms of intelligence that are equally important. By focusing solely on IQ, we may undervalue the contributions and insights that come from emotional and social intelligence. Each person brings unique experiences and skills to the table, offering diverse perspectives and valuable insights.

When we create environments that prioritise emotional intelligence, we enable individuals and groups to flourish. Understanding yourself and how you engage with both your inner world and those around you is crucial for effective teamwork and personal fulfilment.

Developing the ability to self-regulate in a healthy manner, managing emotions without suppressing them, and genuinely connecting with others brings about sustainable and meaningful personal growth.

MINDFULLNESS

Mindfulness is the practice of being aware of and attentive to the present moment, observing stimuli, emotions, and thoughts without judgement. Self-awareness, a big part of EQ, involves being mindful of oneself. We do this by acknowledging and understanding our own emotions, and discerning which ones require action or deeper reflection.

Boundaries are also integral to emotional intelligence. While often viewed as measures to control others behaviour, boundaries are about staying true to oneself and taking personal action. When boundaries are crossed, the key is how you respond, as we can only control our own actions. Recognizing the limits of our control allows us to focus on what we can manage, leading to more effective and peaceful decision-making. Practising emotional intelligence can positively influence both the self and the group, lifting everyone together.

HABITS

I really enjoyed learning about the Fogg Behavior Model. It offers a practical approach to creating long-term habits by breaking down the psychological steps involved. It emphasises targeting specific behaviours, attaching them to existing habits, and keeping them simple. According to Fogg, behaviours are driven by a combination of motivation, ability, and a trigger. No behaviour occurs without a prompt, and using existing behaviours as triggers can be highly effective. For example, you might say, “After I do [existing behaviour], I will do [new behaviour].” Celebrating these small successes helps reinforce the behaviour, making it more likely to continue.

There are two habits that I am committing to to help me during this time. One is for confidence and technical skill building, and the other for clarity and mindfulness.

Firstly, I want to improve my note revision process. I often find myself overwhelmed when working on my blogs. To address this, I plan to review my notes each morning before starting my work. I have a well-established routine of coffee and news. I will replace my news-reading time with note revision to better internalise the material and have it fresh when working on my content. I want to draw insights and solutions to struggles I’m having, along with building confidence with the content.

Secondly, I want to incorporate a 5-10 minute morning meditation or yoga session into my routine. This will help me start my day in a calmer, more centred state, and connect me to my physical needs. Often, when I am deeply focused, I neglect my body's signals for hunger and movement. Integrating this practice after making breakfast and before eating (or while it cooks) will help me stay attuned to my physical self.

Bodyscanning is a technique I use to help me with this, and is a habit I try to stick to, and take action on. Before beginning any task, I check in with myself to ensure I have eaten, hydrated, and moved my body. Reconnecting with my physical self helps me step out of my head and into the present world, signalling to myself that I am safe and my basic needs are met. This grounding process calms me and enables me to make better choices, as I know I am looking after myself.
